BHF Manchester Centre for Heart and Lung Magnetic Resonance Research (MCMR)

The BHF Manchester Centre for Heart and Lung Magnetic Resonance Research (MCMR) is a partnership between the University and the NHS to enable leading edge translational clinical imaging research.

MCMR opened in November 2020 in a purpose-built facility at Wythenshawe Hospital. The 3.1M Centre houses a 3T Siemens Magnetom Vida MR scanner equipped with state-of-the-art clinical sequences for cardiovascular and neuroimaging. Through our collaborative research agreement with Siemens, we have access to pre-commercial ('work-in-progress') sequences as well as the Siemens IDEA pulse programming environment for novel sequence developement.

The scanner is also equipped with a phosphorus coil for metabolic spectroscopy studies and we are currently developing a xenon capability for hyperpolarised gas imaging of the lung

We work with researchers across Manchester and beyond to develop and apply quantitative imaging methods in a range of areas including cardiac, lung, renal, pancreatic, hepatic, neuro and obstetric imaging. Our mission is to translate novel techniques into clinical practice and transform outcomes for patients.

 

Featured studies

MCMR enables a broad range of projects, from novel quantitative methods development to large-scale multi-centre clinical trials. Here are examples of some of our current studies.

COVID-Heart

Demographic, multi-morbidity and genetic impact on myocardial involvement and its recovery from COVID-19: the COVID-HEART study

A significant proportion on patients hospitalised by COVID-19 have evidence of heart muscle injury which associated with adverse outcomes. Led by the University of Leeds, COVID-HEART is a UK, multicentre, prospective, observational, longitudinal cohort study of patients with confirmed COVID-19 and elevated troponin using cardiac MRI to characterize the extent and nature of myocardial involvement in COVID-19 and how it develops over 6 months.

 

Clinical trial registration: ISRCTN 58667920. Registered 04 August 2020.

 

C-MORE

Capturing MultiORgan Effects of COVID-19 (C-MORE)

Although COVID-19 is primarily a respiratory viral illness, emerging data suggests that multiorgan involvement is common in those with moderate-severe infections. Led by the University of Oxford, C-MORE is an observational study that aims to investigate the long-term effects of COVID-19 on the lungs, heart, brain, liver and kidneys using advanced state-of-the art magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) technology.

 

Clinical trial registration: NCT04510025. Registered 12 August 2020.

Recent publications

Research from our Centre features in high profile journals including Nature Medicine and the Journal of the American College of Cardiology. Here you can browse a selection of recent publications by MCMR staff.

Pirfenidone in HFpEF: a randomized phase 2 trial

Full title: Pirfenidone in heart failure with preserved ejection fraction: a randomized phase 2 trial

Authors: Gavin A Lewis, Susanna Dodd, Dannii Clayton, Emma Bedson, Helen Eccleson, Erik B Schelbert, Josephine H Naish, Beatriz Duran Jimenez, Simon G Williams, Colin Cunnington, Fozia Zahir Ahmed, Anne Cooper, Rajavarma Viswesvaraiah, Stuart Russell, Theresa McDonagh, Paula R Williamson, Christopher A Miller

Link: Nat Med . 2021 Aug;27(8):1477-1482 (2021)

In heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF), the occurrence of myocardial fibrosis is associated with adverse outcome. This double-blind, phase 2 trial (NCT02932566) tested the efficacy and safety of pirfenidone, an oral antifibrotic without hempdynamic effect, as a treatment for HFpEF. The primary outcome measure was change in myocardial extracellular volume, from baseline to 52 weeks, as measurend by cardiovascular magnetic resonance. In comparison to placebo, pirfenidone reduced myocardial extracellular volume (between-group difference, -1.21%; 95% confidence interval, -2.12 to -0.31; P = 0.009), meeting the predefined primary outcome.

 

Risk stratification for ventricular arrhythmias and sudden death in nonischemic dilated cardiomyopathy

Full title: Improved Risk Stratification for Ventricular Arrhythmias and Sudden Death in Patients With Nonischemic Dilated Cardiomyopathy

Authors: Andrea Di Marco, Pamela Frances Brown, Joshua Bradley, Gaetano Nucifora, Eduard Claver, Fernando de Frutos, Paolo Domenico Dallaglio, Josep Comin-Colet, Ignasi Anguera, Christopher A Miller, Matthias Schmitt

Link: J Am Coll Cardiol . 2021 Jun 15;77(23):2890-2905 (2021)

This was a retrospective cohort study of consecutive patients with DCM who underwent cardiac magnetic resonance with late gadolinium enhancement (LGE) at 2 tertiary referral centers. In 1,165 patients with a median follow-up of 36 months, LGE was found to be a significant, consistent, and strong predictor of VA or sudden death. A new clinical algorithm integrating LGE and LVEF significantly improved the risk stratification for VA and sudden death, with relevant implications for implantable cardioverter-defibrillator allocation.
